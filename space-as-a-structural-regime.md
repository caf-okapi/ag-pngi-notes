# Space as a Structural Regime

Structural regimes consist of closures that exhibit consistency. Some regimes may be associated with consistent forms of what are commonly described as spatial relations.

Spatial relations may be present wherever structures relate in ways that allow distinctions such as proximity, separation, orientation, or extension. These relations do not require a pre-existing space, nor are they restricted to consistent or stable structures. They may vary, conflict, or fail to persist.

In this framework, space is not a background in which structures appear, but a form of structure associated with relations among structures.

Different structures may be associated with different forms of such relations. No single spatial structure is assumed to be universal, and no foundational preference is assumed for the presence or absence of spatial relations.

Spatial description therefore reflects a way of characterizing certain kinds of relations within some structures, not a foundational stage on which all structure exists.

Some instances of these spatial relations may be associated with regimes, in which such relations persist in a consistent way, as in the familiar sense of space.

Later notes will examine how similar considerations apply to time and how different regimes may exhibit different forms of relational structure.
