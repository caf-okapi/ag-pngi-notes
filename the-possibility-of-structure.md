# The Possibility of Structure

Ontological openness follows from non-governance. Nothing prescribes what must exist, but nothing prohibits structure either.

Once this is recognized, a further consequence becomes clear: if no foundational rules restrict what may appear, the emergence of structure cannot be ruled out in advance.

This possibility does not assume a background arena in which structures unfold. It does not rely on a prior space, domain, or substrate. Such frameworks would already represent forms of foundational organization. Ontological openness removes those assumptions as well.

Nor does this possibility imply randomness or disorder. Ontological openness does not describe chaotic outcomes. It simply removes foundational restrictions on the appearance of structure.

We will refer to this consequence as generativity. Generativity names the possibility that structure can arise when no foundational prescription determines what must exist and no foundational prohibition prevents structure from appearing.

Non-governance permits generativity, but it does not guarantee outcomes. Structures may appear without enduring; they may fail to persist for many possible reasons. Later notes will examine how persistence can arise despite this openness.
