# Compatibility and Incompatibility

Closures persist, but they do not exist in isolation. Under non-governance, multiple closures may be present without coordination.

When closures relate to one another, they need not be compatible. The conditions that allow one closure to persist may not align with those of another. Some closures may coexist without disruption, while others may interfere.

Compatibility refers to the ability of closures to persist without undermining one another. Incompatibility refers to conditions under which closures cannot both persist.

Compatibility does not imply similarity. Closures may differ significantly in structure while still remaining compatible.

Nothing in non-governance requires that closures interact at all. Some closures may never relate or interfere, while others may encounter one another under varying conditions.

These relations do not arise from foundational rules. They follow from the structures of the closures themselves and how those structures relate.

Some closures may remain compatible across a wide range of conditions. Others may be incompatible under even slight variation.

Compatibility and incompatibility therefore play a central role in determining which closures persist and which fail.

Later notes will examine how compatible closures may reinforce one another and how such reinforcement may give rise to more stable and complex structures.
