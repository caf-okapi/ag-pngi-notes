# No Prior Stage

Ontological openness does not assume a background setting in which structures appear.

It is easy to imagine a space in which things occur and a time through which they endure. In many theories this arena is taken as the fundamental stage on which all events unfold.

Non-governance avoids that assumption. A universal space or time would already represent foundational organization. It would define a shared framework within which all structures must appear and relate.

Ontological openness removes this presupposition. The framework does not begin with background space(s), universal time(s), or a predefined domain of possibilities. Such structures may exist, but they cannot be assumed in advance.

If space, time, or other organizing structures are present, they must arise as structures themselves rather than serving as the prior setting in which structure occurs.

Structures may arise directly through generativity or through relations among existing structures. The framework does not assume a single mode of appearance.

Structures are therefore not assumed to appear within a pre-existing stage. Instead, some structures may themselves establish the conditions within which relations become meaningful, or emerge in the relationships between structures — as may occur in structures such as space and time.

Later notes will examine how such structures could arise and how they could persist.
