# Time as a Structural Regime

Structural regimes consist of closures that exhibit consistency. Some regimes may be associated with consistent forms of what are commonly described as temporal relations.

Temporal relations may be present wherever structures relate in ways that allow distinctions such as before, after, or ordering. These relations do not require a pre-existing time, nor are they restricted to consistent or stable structures. They may vary, conflict, or fail to persist.

In this framework, time is not a background through which structures endure, but a form of structure associated with relations among structures.

Different structures may be associated with different forms of such relations. No single temporal structure is assumed to be universal, and no foundational preference is assumed for the presence or absence of temporal relations.

Temporal description therefore reflects a way of characterizing certain kinds of relations within some structures, not a foundational progression through which all structure exists.

Some instances of these temporal relations are associated with regimes, in which such relations persist in a consistent way, as in the familiar sense of time.

Later notes will examine how different regimes may be associated with different forms of such relations and how such relations may be described.
